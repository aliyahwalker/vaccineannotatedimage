The Anatomy of a Driveup COVID-19 Vaccination Site

Vernon Walker getting his first dose of the Pfizer COVID-19 vaccine. Credit: Aliyah Walker

By Aliyah Walker

March 11, 2021

In Connecticut eligible residents can get the COVID-19 vaccine at drive-up vaccination clinics. At these clinics, patients are able to get vaccinated without leaving the comfort of their cars. Licensed medical professionals, including nurses from the surrounding area and combat medics in the states National Guard, come to your car window once you reach their station to give you the vaccine.

Vernon Walker, a Norwalk, Connecticut resident, recently was vaccinated at a drive up vaccination clinic. “It was great, I didn’t have to leave my car and there were enough national guard members around that I felt like if I had a problem or needed help, I’d be able to get it.”

Vaccination clinics are run by The Community Health Center, Inc (CHC). The CHC is "the leading nonprofit healthcare provider in the state of Connecticut". These clinics are successful due to the multiple checkpoints (shown below) that ensure the efficiency of the clinic. Through the help of volunteers and the states National Guard members, some clinics are able to vaccinate up to 240 people a day.

At these checkpoints, Connecticut’s National Guard members confirm your appointment date and time, direct you to the proper vaccination line, go through safety questions, and monitor your symptoms after your vaccine. These checkpoints progress the performance of these clinics.

Walker stated that the checkpoints moved quickly, “The first few checkpoints were very quick you only stopped for a moment.” “The vaccination line and the observation area took the longest, but the wait wasn’t longer than 15 minutes.”

Use the interactive photos below to get an inside look at Stamford, Connecticut's drive up vaccination site:

First Checkpoint

This is the first checkpoint you reach when you get to the drive up vaccination site at Lord & Taylor's in Stamford, CT. Here they check you in for your appointment before they send you to the second checkpoint. At this checkpoint they also put a red or blue block on top of your car. This block lets the fellow members known which shot you are recieving, and if its your first or second dose.

Second Checkpoint

At this checkpoint another national guard member directs you to the proper vaccination line based off of the block thats put on your car.

Third Checkpoint

At this checkpoint you are in the line to get your vaccine. At this point another national guard members comes to your car to ask you a few security questions and scan your id. This line is surrounded by helpful signs to direct any questions and concerns patients may have.

Fourth Checkpoint

The final checkpoint is the observation area. In this area you sit in your car for the required 15 minutes. This checkpoint makes sure that the patient doesn't have any adverse reactions to the vaccine. If you don't have any reactions you're free to leave once your time is up.
